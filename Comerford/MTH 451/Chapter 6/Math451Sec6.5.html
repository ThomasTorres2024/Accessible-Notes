§6.5 The Normal Distribution

The most famous of them all!

Definition).

A random variable $X$ has a $\textbf{normal}(\mu, \sigma)$ distribution if and only if its PDF can be written as:
$$ n(x ; \mu, \sigma)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^{2}},-\infty<x<\infty . $$
$$ \sigma>0 . $$
An important special case is the normal $(0,1)$ distribution, also called the standard normal
$$ n(x ; 0,1)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-\frac{x^{2}}{2}} . $$

Fact).

$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^{2} / 2} d x=\sqrt{2 \pi}$.
Using this it follows that the normal $(0,1)$ distribution has mass 1 and one can also see this for the general normal $(\mu, \sigma)$ since
$$ \begin{array}{ll} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^{2}} d x & z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \\ =\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} \cdot \sigma e^{-z^{2} / 2} d z & d z=\frac{d x}{\sigma} \iff \sigma \cdot dz = dx \end{array} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} & =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{z}{2}} d z \\ & =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} \pi} \cdot \sqrt{2 \pi} \\ & =1 . \end{aligned} $$
Of course, we expect the normal $\left(\mu, \sigma\right)$ distribution should have mean $\mu$ & var $\sigma^{2}$. We show this via the MGF.


Theorem 6.6

The MGF of the normal $(\mu, \sigma)$ distribution is given by
$$ M_{x}(t)=e^{\mu t+\frac{1}{2} \sigma^{2} t^{2}} $$
$Proof).$ By definition
$$ \begin{aligned} & M_{x}(t)=E\left(e^{x t}\right) \\ &=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{x t} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^{2} d x} \\ &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}}\left(-2 \sigma^{2} x t+(x-\mu)^{2}\right) \\ & \end{aligned} $$
We complete the square in the exponent. ie:
$$ \begin{aligned} -2 x t \sigma^{2}+(x-\mu)^{2}= & -2 x t \sigma^{2}+x^{2}-2 \mu x+\mu^{2} \\ = & x^{2}-2\left(\mu+t \sigma^{2}\right) x+\mu^{2} \\ = & x^{2}-2\left(\mu+t \sigma^{2}\right) x+\left(\mu+t \sigma^{2}\right) \\ & -\left(\mu+t \sigma^{2}\right)+\mu^{2} \\ = & \left(x-\left(\mu t+\sigma^{2}\right)\right)^{2} \\ - & \left(\mu^{2}+2 \mu t \sigma^{2}+t^{2} \sigma^{4}\right)+\mu^{2} \end{aligned} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} = & \left(x-\left(\mu t+\sigma^{2}\right)\right)^{2} \\ & -2 \mu t \sigma^{2}-t^{2} \sigma^{4} \end{aligned} $$
Then
$$ \text { en } \begin{aligned} M_{x}(t) & =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\left[\left(x-\left(\mu+t \sigma^{2}\right)\right)^{2}-2 \mu t \sigma^{2}-t^{2} \sigma^{4}\right]} d x \\ & =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\left[x-\left(\mu+t \sigma^{2}\right)\right]^{2}+\mu t+\frac{1}{2} \sigma^{2} t^{2}} d x \\ & =e^{\mu t+\frac{1}{2} \sigma^{2} t^{2}}\left\{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{x-\left(\mu+t \sigma^{2}\right)}{\sigma}\right]^{2}} d x\right\} \end{aligned} $$
The quantity inside the braces is the total mass of a normal with parameters $\mu+t \sigma^{2}$ and $\sigma$ and so is just 1 .
Thus
$$ M_{x}(t)=e^{\mu t+\frac{1}{2} \sigma^{2} t^{2}} \text { as required. } $$

If we differentiate, we get
$$ \begin{aligned} M_{x}^{\prime}(t) & =\left(\mu+\sigma^{2} t\right) e^{\mu t+\frac{1}{2} \sigma^{2} t^{2}} \\ M_{x}^{\prime \prime}(t) & =\sigma^{2} e^{\mu t+\frac{1}{2} \sigma^{2} t^{2}}+\left(\mu+\sigma^{2} t\right)^{2} e^{\mu t+\frac{1}{2} \sigma^{2} t^{2}} \\ & =\left[\left(\mu+\sigma^{2} t\right)^{2}+\sigma^{2}\right] e^{\mu t+\frac{1}{2} \sigma^{2} t^{2}} \end{aligned} $$

If we then set $t=0$ and apply Theorem 4.9, we get
$$ \begin{aligned} & \mu_{1}^{\prime}=M_{x}^{\prime}(0)=\mu \\ & \mu_{2}^{\prime} M_{x}^{\prime \prime}(0)=\mu^{2}+\sigma^{2} \end{aligned} $$

Thus the normal $(\mu, \sigma)$ does indeed have mean $\mu$ and the variance is
$$ \mu_{2}^{\prime}-\left(\mu_{1}^{\prime}\right)^{2}=\mu^{2}+\sigma^{2}-\mu^{2}=\sigma^{2}, $$

Thus. The mean and variance of the normal ( $\mu, \sigma$ ) distribution are $\mu$ and $\sigma^{2}$ respectively.
We can also go the other way and relate the normal $(\mu, \sigma)$ to the standard normal $(0,1)$.

Recall that $X$ is a random variable mith mean $\nu$ & var $\tau^{2}$, then the random variable
$$ Y=a(X+b) $$
has mean $a(v+b)$ and variance $a^{2} \tau^{2}$.
Thus if $x$ has a romal $(\mu, \sigma)$ distr., then
$$ Y=\frac{1}{\sigma}(x-\mu) $$
has mean $\frac{1}{\sigma}(\mu-\mu)=0$ and variance $\frac{1}{\sigma^{2}} \cdot \sigma^{2}=1$.
We have proved the following:

Theorem 6.7)

Theorem 6.7 states that, if $X$ is $\operatorname{normal}(\mu, \sigma)$, then
$$ Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} $$
is a standard normal random variable
This result can be used to calculate probabilities assuciated with normal random variables. The main drawback is that there is no nice formula for the CDF
$$ F(x)=P(x \leq x) $$
of a normal, and we have to resort to tables. Let us denote by
$$ \Phi(x)=P(z \leq x), $$
the CDF of a standard normal $(0,1)$.


Example).

Let $x$ be a normal $(\mu, \sigma)$ random variable
Find the prob. the value of $X$ is within 2 standard deviations of the mean.

Solution).

We want
$$ \begin{aligned} & P(\mu-2 \sigma \leq x \leq \mu+2 \sigma) \\ = & P(-2 \sigma \leq x-\mu \leq 2 \sigma) \\ = & P\left(-2 \leq \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \leq 2\right) \\ = & P(-2 \leq z \leq 2) \\ = & \Phi(2)-\Phi(-2) \\ \approx & .95 . \end{aligned} $$
Notice that this result holds true since $Z$ is a standard normal by theorem 6.7.
