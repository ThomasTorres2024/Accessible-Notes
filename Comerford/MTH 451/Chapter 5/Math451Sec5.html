Probability Distributions

$\S 5.1$ Introduction

In this chapter we introduce several common PDF's associated with discrete and continuous random variables



§5.2 The Discrete Uniform Distribution


If a discrete random variable takes on $k$ values with equal probability we say it has a discrete uniform distribution.


Definition).

We say that a random variable $X$ has a discrete uniform distribution if and only if its PDF is given by
$$ f(x)=\frac{1}{k}, \quad x=x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{k} $$
where $x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{n}$ are all distinct

In this case we have
$$ \begin{aligned} & \mu=E(X)=\sum_{i=1}^{b} x_{i} \cdot \frac{1}{k} \\ & \sigma^{2}=V(X)=\sum_{i=1}^{b}\left(x_{i}-\mu\right)^{2} \cdot \frac{1}{k} . \end{aligned} $$
An important special case is when $x_{i}=i$, $1 \leq i \leq k$.
Here
$$ \mu=\sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{i}{k}=\frac{1}{k} \cdot \frac{k(k+1)}{2}=\frac{k+1}{2} $$
$\mu_{2}^{\prime}=E(X)^{2}=\sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{i^{2}}{k}=\frac{1}{k} \cdot \frac{k(k+1)(2 k+1)}{6}=\frac{(k+1)(2 k+1)}{6}$
So $\sigma^{2}=\mu_{2}^{\prime}-\mu^{2}=\frac{(k+1)(2 k+1)}{6}-\frac{(k+1)^{2}}{4}=\frac{\left(k^{2}-1 \right)}{12}$


§5.3 Bernoulli Distribution

Suppose I flip a biased coin once where the chance is $\theta$ I get a $H$ and $1-\theta$ I get a tail (here $0<\theta<1$ ). This is an example of a Bernoulli randm variable .

Definition).

A random variable $x$ has a Bemoulli $(\theta)$ distribution for some $\theta \in(0,1)$ if and only if its PDF satisfies
$$ f(x ; \theta)= \begin{cases}1-\theta, & x=0 \\ \theta, & x=1 \\ 0 & \text { otherwise } .\end{cases} $$
Can also write this as
$$ f(x ; \theta)=\theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{1-x}, x=0,1 . $$
Thus $f\left(0; \theta\right)=1-\theta$
$$ f(1 ; \theta)=\theta $$
and we can calculate
$$ \begin{aligned} \mu=E(x) & =0 \cdot(1-\theta)+1 \cdot \theta=\theta \\ \sigma^{2}=V(x) & =\mu_{2}^{\prime}-\mu^{2} \\ & =0 \cdot(1-\theta)+1^{2} \cdot \theta-\theta^{2} \\ & =\theta(1-\theta) . \end{aligned} $$
This will be useful to us later when we deal with the next type of PDF, the binomial distribution.


§5.4 Binomial Distribution

Suppose now I flip the same biased coin of the last section $n$ times. What is the probability that in the $n$ flips I get $x$ heads (where $0 \leq x \leq \ n$ )?
Well, the chance of getting $x$ heads and $n-x$ tails in a particular order is
$$ \theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{n-x} $$
and there are $\binom{n}{x}$ suitable orderings. Thus, the probability I get exactly $x$ heads in $n$ flips is
$$ \binom{n}{x} \theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{n-x} . $$

Definition).

A random variable has a binomial ( $n, \theta$ ) distribution $(n \in \mathbb{N}, 0<\theta<1)$ if and only if its PDF is given by
$$ b(x ; n, \theta)=\binom{n}{x} \theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{n-x}, x=0,1,2, \cdots,n . $$
Note that if $n=1,\binom{n}{x}=1$ for $x=0,1$ and
$$ b(x ; 1, \theta)=\theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{1-x}, x=0,1 $$
and we recover the Bemoulli distribution (a) we would expect - why?). Thus the binomial distribution generalizes the Bernoulli distribution.

N.b. the $n$ flips (trials/expenments) must be independent if we are to get a binomial $(n, \theta)$ random variable from $n$ Bernoulli $(\theta)$ random variable.


Example).

The probability of getting $5 \mathrm{H} \& 7 \mathrm{~T}$ in 12 flips of a balanced coin is
$$ \begin{aligned} b\left(5 ; 12, \frac{1}{2}\right) & =\binom{12}{5} \cdot\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{5}\left(1-\frac{1}{2}\right)^{12-5} \\ & =\binom{12}{5} \cdot\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{12} \end{aligned} $$
(Here $n=12, \theta=\frac{1}{2}, x=5$ ).


Example).

Find the probability that 7 out of 10 people recover from a tropical disease if we assume independence, and the probability that any given person recovers is 0.8 .

Here $n=10, \theta=0.8, x=7$ and so the answer is
$$ b(7 ; 10,0.8)=\binom{10}{7}(.8)^{7}(.2)^{.3} \approx .2 $$
Back to coin flips for a while...
If I flip the biased coin $n$ times, then, since we are measuring the total number of successes (heads) in the $n$ trials, if $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}$ are $n$ independent Bernoulli($\theta$) random variables, one for each flip, then
$$ x=x_{1}+x_{2}+\cdots+x_{n} $$
is binomial $(n, \theta)$.

Here the random variables, $X_{1}, \ldots X_{n}$ are said to be independent identically distributed (iid), an important term we will meet later.

In general, random variables $Y_{1}, \cdots, Y_{n}$ are iid If they are independent & have the same PDF.
This representation of a binomial $(n, \theta)$ random variable as the sum of $n$ iid Benoulli( $\theta$ ) random variables is useful for calculating - the mean and variance of this PDF.
Indeed, recalling from the last section that the Benoulli( $\theta$ ) distribution has mean $\theta$ & variance $\theta(1-\theta)$, we get
$$ \begin{aligned} \mu=E(x) & =E\left(x_{1}+\cdots+x_{n}\right) \\ & =\sum_{i=1}^{n} E\left(x_{i}\right) \quad \text { (Theorem 4.3, p. 133) } \\ & =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \theta \quad \text { by iid } \\ & =n \theta \end{aligned} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} \sigma^{2}=V(x) & =V\left(x_{1}+\cdots+x_{n}\right) \\ & =\sum_{i=1}^{n} V\left(x_{i}\right) \quad \text { by corrolary } 4.3, p 154, \text { as } x_{i}, \ldots, x_{n} \text { are independent} \\ & =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \theta(1-\theta) \quad \text{(by iid)} \\ & =n \theta(1-\theta) . \end{aligned} $$
We have proved the following result:

Theorem 5.2).

The mean and variance of the binomial $(n, \theta)$ distribution are
$$ \mu=n \theta, \quad \sigma^{2}=n \theta(1-\theta) . $$
n.b. This can also be calculated directly from
$$ b(x ; n, \theta)=\binom{n}{x} \theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{n-x} $$

Another way to obtain the mean & variance is via the MGF, the Moment Generating Function.


Theorem 5.4)

The MGF of the binomial $(n, \theta)$ distribution is given by
$$ M_{x}(t)=\left[1+\theta\left(e^{t}-1\right)\right]^{n} . $$
$ Proof). $
$$ \begin{aligned} & \quad M_{x}(t)=E\left(e^{x t}\right)\\ & =\sum_{x} e^{x t} f(x) \\ & =\sum_{x=0}^{n} e^{x t}\binom{n}{x} \theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{n-x} \\ & =\sum_{x=0}^{n}\binom{n}{x}\left(\theta e^{t}\right)^{x}(1-\theta)^{n-x} \\ & =\left[\theta e^{t}+(1-\theta)\right]^{n} \text { by the Binomial Theorem. } \\ & =\left[1+\theta\left(e^{t}-1\right)\right]^{n} . \end{aligned} $$
If we differentiate $M_{x}(t)$ thice with respect to $t$, then
$$ \begin{aligned} M_{x}^{\prime}(t)= & n \theta e^{t}\left[1+\theta\left(e^{t}-1\right)\right]^{n-1} \\ M_{x}^{\prime \prime}(t)= & n \theta e^{t}\left[1+\theta\left(e^{t}-1\right)\right]^{n-1} \\ & +n(n-1) \theta^{2} e^{2 t}\left[1+\theta\left(e^{t}-1\right)\right]^{n-2} \\ = & n \theta e^{t}\left(1-\theta+n \theta e^{t}\right)\left[1+\theta\left(e^{t}-1\right)\right]^{n-2} \end{aligned} $$
And if we substitute $t=0$, we get by Theorem 4.9 p. 145
$$ \begin{aligned} & \mu=\left.\frac{d}{d t} M_{x}(t)\right|_{t=0}=n \theta \\ & \mu_{2}^{\prime}=\left.\frac{d^{2}}{d t^{2}} M_{x}(t)\right|_{t=0}=n \theta(1-\theta+n \theta) \end{aligned} $$
So that
$$ \begin{aligned} \sigma^{2}=\mu_{2}^{\prime}-\mu^{2} & =n \theta(1-\theta \operatorname{tn} \theta)-n^{2} \theta^{2} \\ & =n \theta(1-\theta) \end{aligned} $$
as before.


$\S 5.5$ The Negative Binomial and Geometric Distributions

Back to the repeated Bernoulli trials (coin flips).
Suppose we want to know the probability that it will take $x$ trials until we have $k$ successes.
Well, the last trial must be a success and for a Bernoulli $(\theta)$ random variable the probability of this success is $\theta$.
Of the remaining $x-1$ trials which went befere, exactly $k-1$ must be successes and as we saw with the binomial distribution, the probability of this is
$$ \binom{x-1}{k-1} \theta^{k-1}(1-\theta)^{(k-1)-(k-1)} $$
Since the trials are independent, to find the probability that the both success occurs on the $x$th trial, we multiply these probs to get
$$ \begin{aligned} & \theta\binom{x-1}{k-1} \theta^{k-1}(1-\theta)^{(x-1)-(k-1)} \\ = & \binom{x-1}{k-1} \theta^{k}(1-\theta)^{x-k} \end{aligned} $$

Definition).

A random variable $X$ has a negative binomial $(k, \theta)$ distribution if and only if its PDF is given by
$$ \begin{gathered} b^{*}(x ; k, \theta)=\binom{x-1}{k-1} \theta^{k}(1-\theta)^{x-k} \\ x=k, k+1, \ldots \\ (0<\theta<1) . \end{gathered} $$


Example).

If the probability is $0.4$ that a child exposed to a certain contagious disease will catch it, what is the probability that the $10^{\text {th }}$ child exposed will be the $3^{\text {rd }}$ to catch the disease?

Solution).

Here $x=10, k=3, \theta=0.4$ and the answer is
$$ \begin{aligned} b^{*}(10 ; 3, .4) & =\binom{9}{2}(.4)^{3}(.6)^{7} \\ & =.0645 \end{aligned} $$
We have the following relationship between the negative binomial and the binomial.

Theorem 5.5

$$ b^{*}(x ; k, \theta)=\frac{k}{x} \cdot b(k ; x , \theta) . $$
One can show the following

Theorem 5.6

The mean & variance of the negative binomial distribution are
$$ \mu=\frac{k}{\theta} \text { and } \sigma^{2}=\frac{k}{\theta}\left(\frac{1}{\theta}-1\right) \text {. } $$
An important special case of the negative binomial is the Geometric Distribution. Here $k=1$ and we want the probability that the first success occurs on the $x$th trial.


Definition).

A random variable $X$ has a geometric distribution if and only if its PDF is given by
$$ g(x ; \theta)=\theta(1-\theta)^{x-1}, \quad x=1,2,3,\cdots $$
Note that the mean & variance of the geom dism. are
$$ \mu=\frac{1}{\theta}, \quad \sigma^{2}=\frac{1}{\theta}\left(\frac{1}{\theta}-1\right) . $$
by Theorem 5.6 with $k=1$.


Example 5.5).

It the probability is .75 that an applicant for a driver's license will pass the road test on any given try, what is the probability the applicant will pass the test on the 4th attempt?

Solution).

Here $x=4, \theta=.75$ and the answer is
$$ \begin{aligned} g(4 ; .75) & =.75(1-.75)^{4-1} \\ & =.75(.25)^{3} \\ & =.0117 . \end{aligned} $$


$\S 5.7$ The Poisson Distribution

Useful for calculating the number of successes when we have many trials and the chance of success in each trial is low (and the same).
Another way of saying this is that the Poisson distribution is useful for calculating the number of occurrences of rare events (erg. road accidents).

Definition).

A random variable $X$ has a Poisson ( $\lambda$ ) distribution if and only if its PDF is given by
$$ p(x ; \lambda)=\frac{\lambda^{x} e^{-\lambda}}{x!}, x=0,1,2,\cdots $$
Note that $p(x ; \lambda) \geqslant 0$ everywhere and
$$ \begin{aligned} \sum_{x} p(x ; \lambda)=\sum_{x=0}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^{x} e^{-\lambda}}{x!} & =e^{-\lambda} \sum_{x=0}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^{x}}{x!} \\ & =e^{-\lambda} \cdot e^{\lambda}=1 \end{aligned} $$
and so we do have a genuine PDF.

In practice if we have $n$ trials with probability $\theta$ of success in each, then if $n$. is large and $\theta$ small and we set $\lambda=n \theta$, then the prob-of $x$ successes is exactly
$$ \binom{n}{x} \theta^{x}(1-\theta)^{n-x} $$
is well approxmated by
$$ \frac{\lambda^{x} e^{-\lambda}}{x!} \quad(\lambda=n \theta) . $$

Example).

If $2 \%$ of the books at a certain bindery have defective bindings, Find the approximate probability that 5 out of 400 books bound by this bindery have defective bindings.

Solution).

Here $x=5, \lambda=n \theta=400 \times 0.02=8$ and the answer is
$$ p(5 ; 8)=\frac{8^{5} \cdot e^{-8}}{5!} \approx .093 . $$


Example).

Records show that the probability is - $0.00005$ that a car will have a flat tire crossing the bridge to Newport. Use the poision distribution to approximate the binomial probabilities that among 10,000 cars crossing this bridge

a) exactly one will have a flat tire:
b) at most two will have a flat tire.

Solution).

a) Here $x=2, \lambda=10,000(0.00005)=0.5$ and the answer is
$$ p(2 ; .5)=\frac{(.5)^{2} e^{-.5}}{2!} \approx .0758 $$
b) In terms of events:

$$ \begin{align*} \{ \text{ at most 2 cars have a flat tire } \} =\{ \text{ no car 2 car has a flat tire } \} \\ \cup \{ \text{ exactly 1 car has a flat tire } \} \cup \{ \text{ exactly 2 cas have a flat tire } \} \end{align*} $$ Notice that this union is (pairwise) disjoint.
We already know the probability of the third event. For the first two, $x=0,1$ and the respective probabilities using $P(x ; 5)$ are approximately: $0.6065$ and $0.3033$ respectively.
The total probability that at most 2 cars have a flat tire is then approximately:
$$ 0.6065+0.3033+0.0758=0.9856 $$
In order to calculate the mean and variance of the Poisson distribution, we will use its MGF.


Theorem 5.9)

The MGF of the Poisson (d) distribution is
$$ M_{x}(t)=e^{\lambda\left(e^{t}-1\right)} $$
$Proof).$
$$ \begin{aligned} M_{x}(t) & =E\left(e^{t x}\right) \\ & =\sum_{x} e^{x t} \cdot f(x) \\ & =\sum_{x=0}^{\infty} e^{x t} \cdot \frac{\lambda^{x} e^{-\lambda}}{x!} \\ & =e^{-\lambda} \sum_{x=0}^{\infty} \frac{\left(\lambda e^{t}\right)^{x}}{x!} \\ & =e^{-\lambda} \cdot e^{\lambda e^{t}} \\ & =e^{\lambda\left(e^{t}-1\right)} \\ & & \blacksquare \end{aligned} $$
If we now differentiate twice with respect to $t$, we get
$$ \begin{aligned} & M_{x}^{\prime}(t)=\lambda e^{t} e^{\lambda\left(e^{t}-1\right)} \\ & M_{x}^{\prime \prime}(t)=\lambda e^{t} e^{\lambda\left(e^{t}-1\right)}+\lambda^{2} e^{2 t} e^{\lambda\left(e^{t}-1\right)} \end{aligned} $$
Setting $t=0$ and using Theorem $4.9$ (p. 145) as before gives
$$ \begin{aligned} \mu=\mu_{1}^{\prime}=M_{x}^{\prime}(0) & =\lambda \\ \mu_{2}^{\prime}=M_{x}^{\prime \prime}(0) & =\lambda+\lambda^{2} \\ \sigma^{2}=\mu_{2}^{\prime}-\mu^{2} & =\lambda+\lambda^{2}-\lambda^{2} \\ & =\lambda \end{aligned} $$
We have proved the following result:


Theorem 5.8)

The mean and variance of the Poisson $(\lambda)$ distribution are given by
$$ \mu=\lambda, \quad \sigma^{2}=\lambda . $$
Since the expectation of a Poisson ( $\lambda$ ) distribution is $\lambda$, we can uxe it to model the number of (rare) events in a given time interval, given that we already know the average number of events in that much time.


Example).

The average number of trucks arriving on any one day at a truck deport is 12. What is the probability that on a given day fewer than 9 trucks arrive at the depot?

Solution).

Here $\lambda=12$ and the answer is
$$ P(x<9)=\sum_{x=0}^{8} P(x ; 12) \approx .1550 . $$
Suppose we observe on average 2 accidents per hour on a certain stretch of the I-95.

If we watched for 2 hours (or 2 separate 1 hour intervals), we would expect an average of 4 accidents
If we watched for 3 hours, we would expect an average of $t$ accidents, and so on.

In general, if the average number of successes per unit time/area $\alpha$, then on a time interval of length $t$ or a region of area $\alpha$, we would expect $\alpha \cdot t$ successes.
On this interral, we would have a Poisson $(\alpha t)$ random variable with distribution
$$ p(x ; \alpha t)=\frac{e^{-\alpha t}(\alpha t)^{x}}{x!}, x \in 0,1,2 \ldots $$


Example).

A certain kind of sheet metal has on average 5 defects per $10 \mathrm{ft}^{2}$. If we assume a Poisson distribution, what is the probability that a $15 \mathrm{ft}^{2}$ sheet will have at least 6 defects?

Solution).

Let $X=\#$ defects in a $15 \mathrm{ft}^{2}$ sheet. Since the (unit) area is $10\text{ft}^2$, we have that $X$ is Poisson ( $\lambda$ ) with
$$ \lambda=5(1.5)=7.5 . $$
Then
$$ \begin{aligned} P(x \geqslant 6)= & 1-P(x \leq 5) \\ = & 1-P(x=0)-P(x=1)-P(x=2) \\ & -P(x=3)-P(x=4)-P(x=5) \\ = & 1-e^{-7.5} \frac{(7.5)^{0}}{0!}-e^{-7.5} \frac{(7.5)^{1}}{1!}-e^{-7.5} \frac{(7.5)^{2}}{2!} \\ & -e^{-7.5} \frac{(7.5)^{3}}{3!}-e^{-7.5} \frac{(7.5)^{4}}{4!}-e^{-7.5} \frac{(7.5)^{5}}{5!} \\ \approx & 0.7586 \end{aligned} $$

$\S$5.8 Multinomial Distribution

We consider a generalization of the repeated coin flips where there are several possible outcomes.
To be specific, suppose we have $n$ independent trials with $k$ mutually exclusive outcomes whose respective probabilities are $\theta_{1}, \theta_{2}, \ldots, \theta_{k}$ so that:
$$ \sum_{i=1}^{k} \theta_{i}=1 . $$
Suppose we want

      $x_{1}$ outcomes of the first kind
      $x_{2}$ outcomes of the second second...
      $\vdots$
      $x_{k}$ outcomes of the $k$th kind
where of course $x_{1}+x_{2}+\cdots+x_{k}=n$,
The chance of getting these outcomes in a particular order is
$$ \theta_{1}^{x_{1}} \theta_{2}^{x_{2}} \cdots \theta_{k}^{x_{k}} $$
while the number of possible (favorable) orderings is given by the multinomial coefficient
$$ \binom{n}{x_{1}, x_{2}, \cdots x_{k}}=\frac{n!}{x_{1}!x_{2}!\cdots x_{k}!} $$

Definition).

The random variables $X_{1}, \ldots, X_{k}$ have a multinomial $\left(n, \theta_{1}, \ldots, \theta_{k}\right)$ distribution if and only if their joint PDF is given by
$$ f\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{k} ; n, \theta_{1}, \ldots, \theta_{k}\right)=\binom{n}{x_{n}, \cdots, x_{k}} \theta_{1}^{x_{n}} \ldots \theta_{k}^{x_{k}} $$
for $x_{i}=0, \ldots, n$ with $\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i}=n, \sum_{i=1}^{k} \theta_{i}=1$.


Example).

A certain city has 3 TV stations. On Saturday evening Channel 12 has $50 \%$ of the viewing audience, Channel 10 has $30 \%$ and Channel 3 has $20 \%$.
Find the probability that among 8 TV viewers in the city, 5 will be watching Channel 12, 2 will be watching Channel 10, and 1 will be watching Channel 3.

Solution).

Here $n=8, x_{1}=5, x_{2}=2, x_{3}=1 \quad \theta_{1}=0.5, \theta_{2}=0.3, \theta_{3}=.2$ and the answer is
$$ \begin{aligned} & f(5,2,1 ; 8,0.5,0.3,0.2) \\ & \quad=\frac{8!}{5!2!1!}(.5)^{5}(.3)^{2}(.2) \\ & \quad=.0945 \end{aligned} $$
