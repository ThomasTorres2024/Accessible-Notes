$\S$ 4.6 Product Moments

Definition).

The $r$ th and $s$th product moment about the origin of the random variables $X, Y$ denoted $\mu_{r, s}^{\prime}$ is the expectation of $X^{r} \cdot Y^{s}$. If $X, Y$ are discrete, we have
$$ \mu_{r, s}^{\prime}=E\left(X^{r} Y^{s}\right)=\sum_{x} \sum_{y} x^{r} y^{s} f(x, y) $$
$$ r=0,1,2,\cdots \quad s=0,1,2, \cdots $$
and if $X, Y$ are continuous
$$ \mu_{r, s}^{\prime}=E\left(X^{r} Y^{s}\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x^{r} y^{s} \cdot f(x, y) d x d y. $$
$$ r=0,1,2,\cdots \quad s=0,1,2, \cdots $$
As with moments for one random variable, we can also define product moments about the means.


Definition).

Let $X, Y$ be random variables with means $\mu_{x}, \mu_{y}$ respectively. The $r$ th and $s$th product moment about the means of $X, Y$ denoted by $\mu_{r, s}$ is the expected value of $\left(x-\mu_{x}\right)^{r}\left(y-\mu_{x}\right)^{s}$. If $x, y$ are discrete, then
$$ \begin{aligned} & \mu_{r, s}= E\left(\left(x-\mu_{x}\right)^{r}\left(y-\mu_{y}\right)^{s}\right) \\ &=\sum_{x} \sum_{y}\left(x-\mu_{x}\right)^{r}\left(y-\mu_{y}\right)^{s} \cdot f(x, y) \\ & r=0,1,2, \ldots \\ & s=0,1,2, \ldots \end{aligned} $$
and if $X, Y$ are continuous, then
$$ \begin{aligned} & \mu_{r, s}= E\left(\left(x-\mu_{x}\right)^{r} \cdot\left(y-\mu_{y}\right)^{s}\right) \\ &=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\left(x-\mu_{x}\right)^{r}\left(y-\mu_{y}\right)^{s} \cdot f(x, y) d x d y \\ & r=0,1,2, \ldots \\ & r=0,1,2 \ldots \end{aligned} $$
Note that as with one random variable, one needs to consider if these sums and integrals converge.
Note also that
$$ \begin{aligned} & \mu_{x}=E(x)=E\left(x^{1} y^{0}\right)=\mu_{1,0}^{\prime} \\ & \mu_{y}=E(y)=E\left(x^{0} y^{\prime}\right)=\mu_{0,1}^{\prime} \end{aligned} $$
Of special importance is $\mu_{1,1}$ as this is indicative of any relationship (correlation) between $X$ and $Y$.


Definition).

$\mu_{1,1}$ is called the covariance of $X$ and $Y$ and it is denoted by $\sigma_{x y}$, $\operatorname{cov}(X, Y)$, or $C(X, Y)$.
A useful result for calculating covanances in practice.


Theorem 4.11).

We will prove the following: $\quad \sigma_{x y}=\mu_{1,1}^{\prime}-\mu_{x} \mu_{y}$.
$ Proof). \quad \sigma_{x y}=E\left(\left(x-\mu_{x}\right)\left(y-\mu_{y}\right)\right)$ by definition:
$$ \begin{aligned} & =E\left(x y-x \mu_{y}-\mu_{x} y+\mu_{x} \mu_{y}\right) \\ & =E(x y)-\mu_{y} E(x)-\mu_{x} E(y)+\mu_{x} \mu_{y} \\ & =\mu_{1,1}^{\prime}-\mu_{y} \mu_{x}-\mu_{x} \mu_{y}+\mu_{x} \mu_{y} \quad \text{ by definition: } \\ & =\mu_{1,1}^{\prime}-\mu_{x} \mu_{y} \\ && \blacksquare \\ \end{aligned} $$
Remark note the similarity with
$$ \operatorname{var}(x)=\sigma^{2}=\mu_{2}^{\prime}-\mu^{2} \quad \text { (Theorem 4.6) } $$
Indeed if $y=x$, then we get Theorem 4.6 as a special case of Theorem 4.11

Question).

What is the relationship between covariance and independence?

Theorem 4.12

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then
$$ \begin{aligned} E(x y) & =E(x) E(y) \quad \text { and } \\ \sigma_{x y} & =0 . \end{aligned} $$
$ Proof). \quad (D$ is discrete case).

Since $X, Y$ are independent, by definitions we have
$$ f(x, y)=g(x) h(y) $$
Where $g(x), h(y)$ are the marginals in $X, Y$ respectively
Then
$$ \begin{aligned} E(X Y) & =\sum_{x} \sum_{y} x y f(x, y) \\ & =\sum_{x} \sum_{y} x y g(x) \cdot h(y) \text { by independence } \\ & =\left(\sum_{x} x g(x)\right)\left(\sum_{y} y h(y)\right) \\ & =E(X) E(Y) \text { as required. } \end{aligned} $$
Also
$$ \begin{array}{r} \sigma_{x y}=\mu_{1,1}^{\prime}-\mu_{x} \mu_{y}=E(x y)-E(x) E(y) \\ =E(x) E(y)-E(x) E(y)=0 \end{array} $$
So
$X, Y$ independent $\Rightarrow \operatorname{cov}(X, Y)=0$
Howerer, the cenverse is not true in general.


Example).

Suppose $X,Y$ have joint PDF and marginals as below
Then
$$ \begin{aligned} & \mu_{x}=(-1) \cdot \frac{1}{3}+0 \cdot \frac{1}{3}+1 \cdot \frac{1}{3}=0 \\ & \mu_{y}=(-1) \cdot \frac{2}{3}+0 \cdot 0+1 \cdot \frac{1}{3}=-\frac{1}{3} \end{aligned} $$
and
$$ \begin{aligned} \mu_{x y}= & (-1)(-1) \cdot \frac{1}{6}+0(-1) \cdot \frac{1}{3}+1(-1) \cdot \frac{1}{6} \\ & +0+0+0 \\ & +(-1) \cdot 1 \cdot \frac{1}{6}+0+1 \cdot 1 \cdot \frac{1}{6} \\ = & 0 \end{aligned} $$
Then $\sigma_{x y}=\mu_{1,1}^{\prime}-\mu_{x} \mu_{y}=0-0 \cdot\left(-\frac{1}{3}\right)$
$$ =0 . $$
However, $X$ and $Y$ are NOT independent

Eg. $f(0,1)=0$ but
$$ g(0) h(1)=\frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{3}=\frac{1}{9} \neq 0 . $$
Product moments can also be defined for three or more random variables in the obvious way. The results are similar. e.g. One has


Theorem 4.13

If $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}$ are independent, then
$$ E\left(x_{1} x_{2} \cdots x_{n}\right)=E\left(x_{1}\right) E\left(x_{2}\right) \cdots E\left(x_{n}\right) $$
The proof is similar to that for $E(X Y)=E(X) E(Y)$ (Theorem 4.12) of which this is a generalization.
