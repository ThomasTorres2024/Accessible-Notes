§4.5 Moment-Generating Functions

A shortcut to calculating the moments of a distribution.
Defn The moment-generating function (MGF) $M_{X}(t)$ of a random variable $X$ is given by
$$ M_{x}(t)=E\left(e^{t x}\right) $$
When $X$ is discrete, we have by Thm 4.1)
$$ M_{x}(t)=E\left(e^{t \gamma}\right)=\sum_{x} e^{t x} \cdot f(x) . $$
When $X$ is cts, we have (by Thm Lelagain)
$$ M_{x}(t)=E\left(e^{t x}\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{t x} \cdot f(x) d x $$
As an object, $M_{x}(t)$ is a function fram $\mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ given by the rule
$$ t \longmapsto E\left(e^{t x}\right) . $$
Why the name moment-generating fn?
Recall that $e^{t x}$ has McLaurin series expansion
$$ e^{t x}=1+t x+\frac{t^{2} x^{2}}{2!}+\frac{t^{3} x^{3}}{3!}+\cdots+\frac{t^{r}}{r!}+\cdots $$
In the discrete case, we get
$$ \begin{aligned} M_{x}(t) & =E\left(e^{t x}\right) \\ & =\sum_{x} e^{t x} \cdot f(x) \\ & =\sum_{x}\left(1+t x+\frac{t^{2} x^{2}}{2!}+\cdots+\frac{t^{r}}{r!}\right) \cdot f(x) \end{aligned} $$
$$ \begin{array}{r} =\sum_{x} f(x)+t \sum_{x} x f(x)+\frac{t^{2}}{2!} \sum_{x} x^{2} f(x)+\cdots \\ \cdots+\frac{t^{r}}{r!} \sum_{x} x^{r} f(x)+\cdots \end{array} $$
(formal interchange of sums ob)
$$ =I+\mu t+\mu_{2}^{\prime} \frac{t^{2}}{2!}+\cdots+\mu_{r}^{\prime} \frac{t^{\prime}}{r!}+\cdots $$
Thus the in $M_{x}(t)$ itself has a Mclacinn series about $O$ and the coefficient of $t^{r}$ in this series is $\frac{\mu_{r}!}{r!}$ (where $\mu_{r}^{\prime}$ is the $r$-th mament of $r!x$ about 0 ).
A similar argument holds in the cto case.
Recall that if 0 fo $g(t)$ has Mclawin series
$$ g(t)=\sum_{r=0}^{\infty} a_{r} \frac{t^{r}}{r!} $$
about 0 , then
$$ a_{r}=\left.\frac{d^{r} g(t)}{d t^{r}}\right|_{t=0} $$
Applying this to the MGF of $X$ gives.

Theorem 4.9

$$ \left.\frac{d^{r} M_{x}(t)}{d t^{r}}\right|_{t=0}=\mu_{r}^{\prime} $$

Finally, a useful result for calculating MGFs
Thm 4.10 If $a$ and $b$ are constemts, then
  1. $M_{x+a}(t)=E\left(e^{(x+a) t}\right)=e^{a t} M_{x}(t)$
  2. $M_{b x}(t)=E\left(e^{b x t}\right)=M_{x}(b t)$
  3. $M_{\frac{x+a}{b}}(t)=E\left(e^{\left(\frac{x+a}{b}\right) t}\right)=e^{\frac{a}{b} t} M_{x}\left(\frac{t}{b}\right)$
