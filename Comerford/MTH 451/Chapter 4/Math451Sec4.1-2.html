Chapter 4 Mathematical Expectation

$\S$ 4.1,4.2 Introduction and the Expectation of a Random Variable

Basically the expectation of a random variable is the average or expected value of the random variable.

e.g. I play a game in which I roll a (fair) die.

If 1 roll a 1,I win $\$ 2$
If 1 roll a 2 or a 3,I win $\$ 1$
If 1 roll a 4,5 , or 6,I lose $\$ 1$.
On average (i.e. if I play the game many times), I would expect to win.
$$ \begin{aligned} 2 \times \frac{1}{6}+1 \times \frac{1}{3}-1 \times \frac{1}{2} & =\$ \frac{1}{6} \\ & =17 \text{ cents } . \end{aligned} $$
What we did was multiply the value of each outcome by the probability of that outcome and then sum over all possible outcomes.


Definition).

If $x$ is a discrete random variable with PDF $f(x)$, the expected value of $X$ is
$$ E(x)=\sum_{x} x \cdot f(x) $$
If $X$ is a continuous random variable with density $f(x)$, the expected value of $X$ is
$$ E(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) d x $$
N.b. we assume here, of course that the sum and integral are defined.
It is in fact possible that a random variable can have infinite expectation!


Remark

For the physicists among you, note the similarity with centre of mass and also the position operator in quantum mechanics!


Example).

A lot of 12 televisions includes 2 with white cords. If a shipment includes 3 televisions chosen at random, how many can one expect to have white cords?

Solution).

Let $X=$ # televisions shipped with white cords. If we choose $x$ televisions with white cords where $x=0,1,2 \ldots$ then there are $\binom{2}{x}$ ways of choosing the $x$ televisions with white cords
and $\binom{10}{3-x}$ ways of choosing the remaining $3-x$ which don't have white cords.

Since there are $\binom{12}{3}$ ways of choosing the 3 televisions altogether, if we let $f(x)$ be the PDF of $X$, then by Theorem 2.02
$$ f(x)=\frac{\binom{2}{x}\binom{10}{3-x}}{\binom{12}{3}}, x=0,1,2 $$
and $f(x)=0$ otherwise.

We can make a table:
$x$ 0 1 2
$f(x)$ $\frac{6}{11}$ $\frac{9}{22}$ $\frac{1}{22}$
Then
$$ \begin{aligned} E(x)= & 0 \cdot P(x=0)+1 \cdot P(x=1) \\ & +2 \cdot P(x=2) \\ = & 0 \cdot f(0)+1 \cdot f(1)+2 \cdot f(2) \\ = & 0 \cdot \frac{6}{11}+1 \cdot \frac{9}{22}+2 \cdot \frac{1}{22}=\frac{1}{2} \end{aligned} $$
Note that this is not a whole number!


Example).

A continuous random variable $X$ has PDF
$$ f(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{l} \frac{4}{\pi\left(1+x^{2}\right)}, 0<x<1 \\ 0 \text { elsewhere. } \end{array}\right. $$
Find $E(X)$.
$$ \begin{aligned} E(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) d x & =\frac{4}{\pi} \int_{0}^{1} \frac{x}{1+x^{2}} d x \\ & =\frac{2}{\pi} \int_{0}^{1} \frac{2 x}{1+x^{2}} d x \\ & \text{Now, let } u=1+x^2 \implies du=2x\text{d}x \\ & =\frac{2}{\pi} \int_{1}^{2} \frac{d u}{u} \\ & =\frac{2}{\pi}[\ln |u|]_{1}^{2} \\ & =\frac{2}{\pi}(\ln 2-\ln 1) \\ & =\frac{2}{\pi} \ln 2 \\ & =\frac{\ln 4}{\pi} \approx 0.4413 \end{aligned} $$
Often we want to know the expectation of a function $g(x)$ of a random variable. $X$ (note that $g(X)$ is just another random variable!).


Theorem 4.1

If $x$ is a discrete random variable wth PDF $f(x)$, then the expected value of $g(x)$ is given by
$$ E(g(x))=\sum_{x} g(x) \cdot f(x) . $$
If $X$ is a continuous random variable with density $f(x)$, then the expected value of $g(x)$ is given by
$$ E(g(x))=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) \cdot f(x) d x $$
$Proof).$
We will only do this in the simplest case when $X$ is discrete and takes on finitely many values and $g$ is only finite - to -one.
Suppore that $g(x)$ takes on the value $g_i$ when $x=x_{i 1}, x_{i 2} \ldots, x_{i n_i}$
Then
$$ \begin{aligned} P\left(g(x)=g_{i}\right) & =f\left(x_{i 1}\right)+\cdots+f\left(x_{i n_{i}}\right) \\ & =\sum_{j=1}^{n_{i}} f\left(x_{i j}\right) \end{aligned} $$
and if $g(x)$ takes on the values $g_{1}, g_{2}, \ldots, g_{m}$, then
$$ \begin{aligned} E(g(x)) & =\sum_{i=1}^{m} g_{i} P\left(g(x)=g_{i}\right) \\ & =\sum_{i=1}^{m} g_{i} \sum_{j=1}^{n_i} f\left(x_{i j}\right) \\ & =\sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{i=1}^{n_i} g_{i} f\left(x_{i j}\right) \end{aligned} $$
$$ =\sum_{x} g(x) \cdot f(x) $$
Where this summation is over all possible values of $X$.


Example).

If $X$ is the number of points rolled with a balanced die, then if $g(x)=2 x^{2}+1$
$$ \begin{aligned} E(g(x)) & =\sum_{x=1}^{6}\left(2 x^{2}+1\right) \cdot \frac{1}{6} \\ & =\left(2 \cdot 1^{2}+1\right) \cdot \frac{1}{6}+\cdots+\left(2 \cdot 6^{2}+1\right) \cdot \frac{1}{6} \\ & =\frac{94}{3} . \end{aligned} $$


Example).

If $X$ is a continuous random variable. with PDF
$$ f(x)= \begin{cases}e^{-x}, & x>0 \\ 0, & x \leq 0\end{cases} $$

and $g(x)=e^{3x/14}$, find $E(g(x))$.

Solution).


By Theorem 4.1,
$$ \begin{aligned} E\left(e^{3x / 4}\right) & =\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{3 x / 4} e^{-x} d x \\ & =\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-x / 4} d x \\ & =\lim _{M \rightarrow \infty}\left[\int_{0}^{M} e^{-x / 4} d x\right] \\ & =\lim _{M \rightarrow \infty}\left[-4 e^{-x / 4}\right]_{0}^{M} \\ & =\lim _{M \rightarrow \infty}\left(-4 e^{-M / 4}-(-4)\right)=4 \end{aligned} $$


Useful facontinuous

Theorem 4.2).

If $a, b$ are constants, then
$$ E(a X+b)=a E(X)+b . $$
$Proof)$. Discrete case - see book for continuous case.

Let $g(x)=a x+b$. Then by Theorem 4.1
$$ \begin{aligned} & E(a X+b)=\sum_{x}(a x+b) f(x) d x \\ & \begin{array}{l} =a \sum_{x} x f(x) d x+b \sum_{x} f(x) d x \\ =a \sum_{x} x f(x) d x+b(1) \quad \text{ (as f is just a PDF!) } \\ =a E(x)+b \end{array} \\ \end{aligned} $$


Corollary 4.1)

If $a$ is a constant, then
$$ E(a X)=a E(X) $$

Corollary 4.2)

If $b$ is a constant, then $E(b)=b \quad$ (here $b$ is regarded as a constamt random variable which has only 1 value)


Theorem 4.3)

If $c_{1}, \ldots, c_{n}$ are constants, then
$$ E\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} c_{i} g_{i}(x)\right]=\sum_{i=1}^{n} c_{i} E\left(g_{i}(x)\right) $$
$Proof).$ For the continuous case see book. For discrete case, by Theorem 4.1, if $g(x)=\sum_{i=1}^{n} g_{i}(x)$,
$$ \begin{aligned} & E\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} c_{i} g_{i}(x)\right]=E(g(x)) \\ &=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) f(x) d x \\ &=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} c_{i} g_{i}(x)\right) f(x) d x \\ &=\sum_{i=1}^{n} c_{i}\left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g_{i}(x) f(x) d x\right) \\ &=\sum_{i=1}^{n} c_{i} E\left(g_{i}(x)\right) \text { by Theorem 4.1 } \\ & \text { (backwards) } \end{aligned} $$


Example).

If $X$ is a continuous random variable with density
$$ f(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{cc} 2(1-x), & 0<x<1 \\ 0 & \text { elsewhere } \end{array}\right. $$
a) show that
$$ E\left(X^{r}\right)=\frac{2}{(r+1)(r+2)}, \quad r \neq-1,-2 $$
b) Use this to evaluate
$$ E\left((2 x+1)^{2}\right) $$

Solution).


a)
$$ \begin{aligned} E\left(X^{r}\right) & =\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x^{r} \cdot f(x) d x \\ & =\int_{0}^{1} x^{r} \cdot 2(1-x) d x \\ & =2 \int_{0}^{1}\left(x^{r}-x^{r+1}\right) d x \end{aligned} $$
$=2\left(\frac{1}{r+1}-\frac{1}{r+2}\right)=\frac{2}{(r+1)(r+2)}$.
b)
$$ \begin{aligned} & E\left((2 x+1)^{2}\right)=E\left(4 x^{2}+4 x+1\right) \\ & =4 E\left(x^{2}\right)+4 E(x)+1 \\ & =4 \cdot \frac{2}{(2+1)(2+2)}+4 \cdot \frac{2}{(1+1)(1+2)}+1 \\ & \text { by a) with } r=2, r=1, \\ & =\frac{8}{12}+\frac{8}{6}+1=3 . \end{aligned} $$


Example).

Show that
$$ E\left((a x+b)^{n}\right)=\sum_{i=0}^{n}\binom{n}{i} a^{n-i} b^{i} E\left(x^{n-i}\right) $$

Solution).

Since $(a x+b)^{n}=\sum_{i=0}^{n}\binom{n}{i}(a x)^{n-i} b^{i}$,
$$ \begin{aligned} E\left((a x+b)^{n}\right) & =E\left[\sum_{i=0}^{n}\binom{n}{i} a^{n-i} b^{i} x^{n-i}\right] \\ & =\sum_{i=0}^{n}\binom{n}{i} a^{n-i} b^{i} E\left(x^{n-i}\right) . \end{aligned} $$
Can also extend expectation to the situation of one random variable which is a function of two or more other random variables which have a joint density.


Theorem) 4.4

If $X$ & $Y$ are discrete random variables with joint PDF $f(x, y)$, the expected value of $g(X, Y)$ (where $g$ is a function from $\mathbb{R}^{2} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ and $g(X, Y)$ a random variable) is
$$ E(g(x, y))=\sum_{x} \sum_{y} g(x, y) \cdot f(x, y) . $$
If $x, y$ are continuous with joint density $f(x, y)$, the expected value of $g(X, Y)$ is
$$ E(g(x, y))=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x, y) \cdot f(x, y) d x d y . $$
Similar statements exist for functions of three or more variables.


Example).

For the tablets (yet again), find $E(x+y)$.
Recall $X, Y=$ #aspirin, sedative resp when 2 tablets are taken from a bottle containing 3 aspirin, 2 sedative and 4 laxative tablets. $X \& Y$ had joint PDF.
0 1 2
0 $\frac{1}{6}$ $\frac{1}{3}$ $\frac{1}{12}$
1 $\frac{2}{9}$ $\frac{1}{6}$
2 $\frac{1}{36}$
Then
$$ \begin{aligned} E(X+Y)= & \sum_{x=0}^{2} \sum_{y=0}^{2}(x+y) f(x, y) \\ = & (0+0) \cdot \frac{1}{6}+(0+1) \cdot \frac{2}{9}+(0+2) \cdot \frac{1}{36} \\ & +(1+0) \cdot \frac{1}{3}+(1+1) \cdot \frac{1}{6}+(2+0) \cdot \frac{1}{12} \\ = & \frac{10}{9} \end{aligned} $$


Example).

If $x, y$ are continuous with joint PDF
$$ f(x, y)= \begin{cases}\frac{2}{7}(x+2 y), & 0<x<1,0<y<2 \\ 0 & \text { elsewhere }\end{cases} $$
find the expected value of $g(x, y)=x / y^{3}$

Solution).

$$ \begin{aligned} & E\left(x / y^{3}\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{x}{y^{3}} \cdot f(x, y) d x d y \\ & =\int_{1}^{2} \int_{0}^{1} \frac{x}{y^{3}} \cdot \frac{2(x+2 y)}{7} d x d y \\ & =\frac{2}{7} \int_{1}^{2} \int_{0}^{1}\left(\frac{x^{2}}{y^{2}}+\frac{2 x}{y^{2}}\right) d x d y \\ & =\frac{2}{7} \int_{1}^{2}\left(\frac{1}{3 y^{3}}+\frac{1}{y^{2}}\right) d y \\ & =\frac{15}{84} . \end{aligned} $$
