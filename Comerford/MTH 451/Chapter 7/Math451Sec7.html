$\S$ 7. The Weak Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem

Both theorems deal with the situation of what happens in the limit for large numbers of repeated, independent, trials.
Let $X_{1}, X_{2}, \ldots, X_{n}, \ldots$ be an infinite sequence of independent random variables each with the same mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^{2}$ (e.g.
$X_{1}, X_{2} \ldots, X_{n}$ i.i.d with mean $\mu$, var $\sigma^{2}$ ).
Set $S_{n}=X_{1}+X_{2}+\cdots+X_{n}, n \geq 1$ (sum) and $\bar{X}_{n}=\frac{S_{n}}{n}=\frac{X_{1}+ \cdots X_{n}}{n}, n \geq($ average $)$.
Then
$$ \begin{aligned} E\left( \bar{X_n} \right) & =E\left(\frac{X_{1}+\ldots+X_{n}}{n}\right) \\ & =\frac{1}{n} E\left(S_{n}\right) \\ & =\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i} E\left(X_{i}\right) \quad \text { Theorem 4.14, p.153 } \\ & =\frac{1}{n} \cdot \sum_{i} \mu \\ & =\frac{n \mu}{n}=\mu . \end{aligned} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} V\left(\bar{X}_{n}\right) & =V\left(\frac{X_{1}+\ldots+X_{n}}{n}\right) \\ & =V\left(\frac{X_{1}}{n}+\cdots+\frac{X_{n}}{n}\right) \\ & =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{n^{2}} V\left(X_{i}\right) \quad \text{by independence and Corrolary 4.3 p. 154} \\ & =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{n^{2}} \sigma^{2} \\ & =\frac{n \sigma^{2}}{n^{2}}=\frac{\sigma^{2}}{n} . \end{aligned} $$
The important facts here are that
  • $\bar{X}_{n}$ has the same mean $\mu$ as all the $X_{i}^{\prime}$ s.
  • $V\left(\bar{X}_{n}\right) \rightarrow 0$ as $n \rightarrow \infty$.
If we apply Chebyshev to $\bar{X}_{n}$, then for any $\varepsilon>0$
$$ \begin{aligned} P\left(\left|\bar{X}_{n}-\mu\right| \geq \varepsilon\right) & \leq \frac{V\left(\bar{X}_{n}\right)}{\varepsilon^{2}} \\ & =\frac{\sigma^{2}}{n \varepsilon^{2}} \rightarrow 0 \text { as } n \rightarrow \infty . \end{aligned} $$
We have proved the following result:


Theorem).

Weak Law of Large Numbers (WLLN)
If $X_{1}, X_{2}, \ldots, X_{n}, \ldots$ are independent with the same the mean $\mu$ & fte. var $\sigma^{2}$, then for any $\varepsilon>0$
$$ \begin{aligned} \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} P\left(\left|\bar{X}_{n}-\mu\right|>\varepsilon\right) & =0 . \\ & \text { (Book Theorem 8.2, P. 267) } \end{aligned} $$
This is an example of convergence in probability. There are other versions of this result which involve other types of convergence (the strong Law of Large Numbers).
The conclusion of the WLLN is actually very intuitive. Basically it says that one converges to the average over more and more repeated trials.

e.g. If I keep flipping a coin, I expect that the proportions of heads & tails get closer and closer to $50 \%$ the more I flip.
Can use the estimate
$$ P\left(\left|\bar{X}_{n}-\mu\right| \geq \varepsilon\right) \leq \frac{\sigma^{2}}{n \varepsilon^{2}} $$
to see how fast $\bar{X}_{n}$ converges to the mean $\mu$.


Example).

I flip a fair coin repeatedly. How many times do I need to flip in order to be at least $95 \%$ sure the proportions of heads and tails are within $1 \%$ of $50 \%$
Here each $X_{i}$ is a Benoulli $\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)$ random variable with mean $\frac{1}{2}$ & variance $\frac{1}{2}\left(1-\frac{1}{2}\right)=\frac{1}{4}$.
Here $\varepsilon=.01$
Want
$$ p\left(\left|\bar{X}_{n}-.5\right| \geq .1\right) \leq \frac{\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^{2}}{n(.01)^{2}}<.05 $$
ie $\quad \frac{\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^{2}}{n(.01)^{2}} \leq .05$
So $n \geq \frac{\left(\frac{1}{4}\right)^{2}}{.05(.01)^{2}}=12,500$ flips.
One can think of the random variables $\bar{X}_{n}=\frac{S_{n}}{n}$ in WLLN as being normalized so that the means $E\left(\bar{X}_{n}\right)=\mu$ are constant.
Now suppose we consider the random variables:
$$ Z_{n}=\frac{\bar{X}_{n}-\mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} $$
Then $E\left(Z_{n}\right)=\frac{1}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}\left(E\left(\bar{X}_{n}\right)-\mu\right)=0$ and
$$ \begin{aligned} V\left(Z_{n}\right) & =E\left(\left(Z_{n}-E\left(Z_{n}\right)^{2}\right)\right. \\ & =E\left(Z_{n}^{2}\right) \quad \text { as } \quad E\left(Z_{n}\right)=0 \\ & =E\left(\left(\frac{\bar{X}_{n}-\mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}\right)^{2}\right) \end{aligned} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} & =\frac{1}{\sigma^{2} / n} E\left(\left(\bar{X}_{n}-\mu\right)^{2}\right) \\ & =\frac{1}{\sigma^{2} / n} \cdot V\left(\bar{X}_{n}\right) \quad \text { by definitions of variance } \\ & =\frac{\sigma^{2} / n}{\sigma^{2} / n}=1 . \end{aligned} $$
Thus the random variables $Z_n$ can be thought of as a way of normalizing the sums $S_{n}$ so that they all have mean 0 and variance 1 .
Not only are these the mean and variance of the standard normal, but the sequence of random variables converges to a standard normal in the following sense


Theorem).

Central Limit Theorem.
If $X_{1}, X_{2} \ldots, X_{n}, \ldots$ are i.i.d random variables with the same fte mean $\mu$ & the same fte variance $\sigma^{2}$, then for any $z \in \mathbb{R}$
$$ \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} P\left(Z_{n} \leq z\right)=\Phi(z) $$
or
$$ \lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} P\left(\frac{\bar{X}_{n}-\mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \leq z\right)=\Phi(z) $$
Where $\Phi(z)$ is the CDF of the standard normal.
The convergence in the statement of this theorem is an example of convergence in distribution (also called weak convergence).

Fact).

A binomial $(n, \theta)$ random variable with $n$ large is approximately a normal $(n \theta, n \theta(1-\theta))$ random variable in the sense of distributions.
Picture


Example).

Suppose $X$ is a binomial $(n, \theta)$ random variable Then as we saw carlier
$$ \begin{aligned} & X=X_{1}+\cdots+X_{n} \quad \text { where } X_{1}, \cdots, X_{n} \\ & \text { are iid Bernoulli (} \theta \text {) } \\ & \text { random variables } \end{aligned} $$
From earlier (Theorem 5.2.p.168), $x$ has mean $\mu=n \theta, \&$ variance $\sigma^{2}=n \theta(1-\theta)$.
Then if $n$ is large, by CLT
$$ Z=\frac{X-n \theta}{\sqrt{n \theta(1-\theta)}} $$
is approximately a normal $(0,1)$ in distribution
This then means that
$$ X=\sqrt{n \theta(1-\theta)} Z+n \theta $$
is approximately a normal $(n \theta, n \theta(1-\theta))$ in distribution


Example).

Let $x$ be a binomial $\left(30, \frac{1}{6}\right)$ random variable
Use the CLT to find the approximate probability that $x=3$.

$x$ has mean $30 \times \frac{1}{6}=5$ and variance. $30 \times \frac{1}{6} \times\left(1-\frac{1}{6}\right)=\frac{25}{6}$
By CLT $X$ is approximately a normal $\left(5, \frac{25}{6}\right)$ and $\frac{x-5}{\sqrt{25 / 6}}$ is therefere approximately a standard normal normal.
Then
$$ \begin{aligned} & P(X=3)=P(2.5 \leq x \leq 3.5) \\ & \quad(x \text { is discrete }) \\ \\ & =P\left(\frac{2.5-5}{\sqrt{25 / 6}} \leq \frac{x-5}{\sqrt{25 / 6}} \leq \frac{3.5-5}{\sqrt{25 / 6}}\right) \\ & \quad P(-2.69 \leq Z \leq-.73) \text { where } z \\ & \text { is a standard normal } \end{aligned} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} & =\Phi(-.73)-\Phi(-2.69) \\ & \approx .2291 . \end{aligned} $$
