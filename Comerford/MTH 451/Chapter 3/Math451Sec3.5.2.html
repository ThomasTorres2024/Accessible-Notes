$\S$ 3.5.2 Multivariate Distributions

We may also consiter the cave of two continuous random variables

Definition).


A bivariate function with two values $f(x, y)$ defined on $\mathbb{R}^{2}$ is called a joint probability density function of the continuous random variables $X$ and $Y$ if and only if
$$ P((x, y) \in A)=\iint_{A} f(x, y) d x d y $$
for any region $A$ in the $x y$-plane. Analogous to Theorem 3.5.1 for one continuous random variable, we have


Theorem 3.8

A bivariate function can serve as a joint PDF a pair of continuous random variables $X, Y$ if its values $f(x, y)$ satisfy
  1. $f(x, y) \geq 0,-\infty<x, y<\infty$
  2. $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y) d x d y=1$.


Example).

Given the joint PDF
$$ f(x, y)= \begin{cases}\frac{3}{5} x(y+x), & 0<x<1,0<y<2 \\ 0, & \text { elsewhere }\end{cases} $$
of two random variables $X, Y$, find $P((x, y) \in A)$ where $A$ is the region
$$ A=\left\{(x, y): 0<x<\frac{1}{2}, 1<y<2\right\} . $$

Solution).

$$ \begin{gathered} P((x, y) \in A)=P\left(0<x<\frac{1}{2}, 1<y<2\right) \\ =\int_{1}^{2} \int_{0}^{\frac{1}{2}} \frac{3}{5} x(y+x) d x d y \\ =\int_{1}^{2}\left[\frac{3 x^{2} y}{10}+\frac{3 x^{3}}{15}\right]_{0}^{\frac{1}{2}} d y \\ =\int_{1}^{2}\left(\frac{3 y}{40}+\frac{1}{40}\right) d y=\left[\frac{3 y^{2}}{80}+\frac{y}{40}\right]_{1}^{2}=\frac{11}{80} \end{gathered} $$
We can also define a joint CDF for two continuous random variables


Definition).

If $X, Y$ are continuous random variables, the function given by
$$ \begin{align} F(x, y)=P(X \leq x, Y \leq y)=\int_{-\infty}^{y} \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(s, t) d s d t\\ -\infty < x,y < \infty \end{align} $$
Where $f(x, y)$ is the value of a joint PDF of $X, Y$ is called the joint cumulative distribution function of $X$ and $Y$.
Similarly to the univariate case:



Theorem 3.5.2

If $F(x, y)$ is the value of the joint CDF of two continuous random variables $X \& Y$ at $(x, y)$, then

$$ a) \lim_{\substack{x\to -\infty\\ y\to -\infty}} F(x, y)=0 \quad \quad b) \lim_{\substack{x\to \infty\\ y\to \infty}} F(x, y)=1$$
$$c). \text{ if } a<b \text{ and } c<d \text{ then } F(a, c) \leq F(b, d)$$
Similarly to $f(x)=\frac{d F(x)}{d x}$ for one continuous random variable, for two continuous random variables we have
$$ f(x, y)=\frac{\partial^{2} F(x, y)}{\partial x \partial y} $$

Whenever these partial derivatives exist.
So as before
$$ \text { Joint CDF } \xrightarrow{\frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial x \partial y}} \text { Joint pdf. } $$
As in $\S 3.4$, we let $f(x, y)=0$ whenever the above relationship doesn't hold.


Example).

If the joint PDF of $x \& y$ is given by
$$ f(x, y)= \begin{cases}x+y, & 0<x, y<1 \\ 0, & \text { elsewhere }\end{cases} $$
find the joint CDF of these two random variables's.

Solution).

If either $x<0$ or $y<0$, then $F(x, y)=0$.

e.g. If $x<0$
$$ \begin{aligned} F (x, y) & =\int_{-\infty}^{y} \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(s, t) d s d t \\ & =\int_{-\infty}^{y} 0 d t \quad \text { as } \quad f(s, t)=0 \text { as } \\ & =0 \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad s \leq x < 0 \end{aligned} $$
(Region 0 )
If $0<x<1,0<y<1$ (Region I),
$F(x, y)=\int_{0}^{y} \int_{0}^{x}(s+t) d s d t=\frac{1}{2} x y(x+y)$.
If $1<x, \quad 0<y<1 \quad($ Region II ),
$F(x, y)=\int_{0}^{y} \int_{0}^{1}(s+t) d s d t=\frac{1}{2} y(y+1)$

If $0<x<1,1<y \quad$ (Region III),
$F(x, y)=\int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{x}(s+t) d s d t=\frac{1}{2} x(x+1)$

If $1<x , 1<y \quad$ (Region IV),
$F(x, y)=\int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1}(s+t) d s d t=1$.
So
$$ F(x, y)= \begin{cases}0, & x \leq 0, y \leq 0 \\ \frac{1}{2} x y(x+y), & 0<x<1,0<y<1 \\ \frac{1}{2} y(y+1), & 1<x, 0<y<1 \\ \frac{1}{2} x(x+1), & 0<x<1,1<y \\ 1, & 1 \leq x, 1 \leq y .\end{cases} $$
N.b. Since $F$ is continuous everywhere, it doesn't matter just which region we say the boundary lines (e-g. $x=1$ ) belong to.


Example).

Find the joint PDF of the two random variables $X$ and $Y$ whose joint CDF is given by
$$ F(x, y)= \begin{cases}\left(1-e^{-x}\right)\left(1-e^{-y}\right), x, y>0 \\ 0 & \text { otherwise }\end{cases} $$
Also use the joint PDF to find
$$ P(1<x<3,1<y<2) . $$

Solution


Partial differentration gives
$$ \frac{\partial^{2} F}{\partial x \partial y}=e^{-(x+y)} \text { if } x, y>0 $$
and $\frac{\partial^{2} F}{\partial x \partial y}=0$ if $x<0$ or $y<0$.
We can then set $f(x, y)=0$ on the remaining points ( $x=0$ or $y=0$ ) to get

All the definitions in this section can be generalized to the multivariate case where we have $n$ random variables

The joint PDF of $n$ discrete random variables is given by

$$ f\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)=P\left(X_{1}=x_{1}, x_{2}=x_{2}, \ldots, X_{n}=x_{n}\right) $$

for each $n$-tuple $\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)$ within the range of the random variables

$$ f(x, y)= \begin{cases}e^{-(x+y)}, & x>0, y>0 \\ 0 & \text { othernise. }\end{cases} $$
Then, by integration
$$ \begin{aligned} P(1<x<3,1<y<2) & \\ =\int_{1}^{2} \int_{1}^{3} e^{-(x+y)} d x d y & =\left(e^{-1}-e^{-3}\right)\left(e^{-1}-e^{-2}\right) \\ & =e^{-2}-e^{-3}-e^{-4}+e^{-5} \\ & \approx 0.074 \end{aligned} $$
In terms of multivariable calculus, we can think of f(x,y) as a surface and probability corresponds to finding the volume under the surface on a certain region

The joint CDF is given by

$$ F\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)=P\left(x_{1} \leq x_{1}, x_{2} \leq x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n} \leq x_{n}\right) $$

for $-\infty<x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}<\infty$.


Example).

If the joint PDF of 3 discrete random variables is given by
$$ f(x, y, z)=\frac{(x+y) z}{63}, \begin{aligned} & x=1,2 \\ & y=1,2,3 \\ & z=1,2 \end{aligned}, $$
find $P(x=2, y+z \leq 3)$.

Solution).

.
$$ \begin{aligned} P(x=2, x+z & \leq 3)=f(2,1,1)+f(2,1,2)+f(2,2,1) \\ & =\frac{3}{63}+\frac{6}{63}+\frac{4}{63} \\ & =\frac{13}{63} \end{aligned} $$

In the continuous case, prbaibilities are again obtained by integrating a pdf and
$$ P\left(\left(X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n}\right) \in A\right)=\iint \cdots-\int f\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{n} $$
for any region $A \subset \mathbb{R}^{n}$.

The joint CDF is given by

$$ \begin{aligned} F\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right) & =P\left(x_{1} \leq x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n} \leq x_{n}\right) \\ & =\int_{-\infty}^{x_{n}} \cdots \int_{-\infty}^{x_{2} x_{1}} \int_{-\infty} f\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, \ldots, t_{n}\right) d t_{1} d t_{2} \cdots d t_{n}, \\ & -\infty<x_{1, \ldots}, x_{n}<\infty \end{aligned} $$

Also partial differentiation givesL

$$ f\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)=\frac{\partial^{n}}{\partial x_{1} \partial x_{2} \ldots \partial x_{n}} F\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{n}\right) $$

whenever these partial derivatives exist.


Example).

If the trivariate probabiility density of $x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}$ is given by

$$ f\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}\right)=\left\{\begin{array}{l} \left(x_{1}+x_{2}\right) e^{-x_{3}}, \quad 0<x_{1}<1,0<x_{2}<1,0<x_{3} \\ 0 \quad \text { otherwise } \end{array}\right. $$
find $P\left(\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}\right) \in A\right.$. Where
$$ A=\left\{\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}\right) ; 0<x_{1}<\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}<x_{2}<1, x_{3}<1\right\} $$

Solution).

$$ \begin{gathered} P\left(\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}\right) \in A\right)=P\left(0<x_{1}<\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}<x_{2}<1, x_{3}<1\right) \\ =\int_{0}^{1} \int_{\frac{1}{2}}^{1} \int_{0}^{\frac{1}{2}}\left(x_{1}+x_{2}\right) e^{-x_{3}} d x_{1} d x_{2} d x_{3} \\ =\int_{0}^{1} \int_{\frac{1}{2}}^{1}\left(\frac{1}{8}+\frac{x_{2}}{2}\right) e^{-x_{3}} d x_{2} d x_{3} \\ =\int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{4} e^{-x_{3}} d x_{3}=\frac{1}{4}\left(1-e^{-1}\right) \approx 0.158 \end{gathered} $$
