$\S$ 3.5.1 Multivariate Distributions

One can define many dif and only iferent random variables over the same sample space.
e.g.. Roll two dice, one red, one green. One random variable. might be the product of the numbers displayed, another their sum, another the sine of their dif and only iference, and so on.
We consrider multivariate distributions where we have several random variable's defined on the same sample space and we are interested in all of their values simultaneously.

We shall first look at the bivariate case with two random variable's defined on the same sample space. (with just one random variable., we have the univariate case).


Example.)

Two tablets are selected at random from a bottle which contains 3 aspirin, 2 sedative, and 4 laxaitive tablets. If $X$ & $Y$ are respectively the numbers of aspinin and sedative tablets included among the two drawn from the bottle, find the probabilities associated with all possible values of $X \& Y$.

Solution).

Possible pairs are
$$ (0,0),(0,1),(1,0),(1,1),(0,2),(2,0) . $$
To find the probability for $(1,0)$, for example, we are drawing 1 aspirin $O$ sedatre & 1 laxitive tablets.
Aspirin can be drawn in $\binom{3}{1}$ ways
Sedative - $\binom{2}{0}$ ways
Laxative - $\binom{4}{1}$ ways
This gives a total of
$$ \binom{3}{1}\binom{2}{0}\binom{4}{1}=12 $$

Ways of drawing 1 aspirin & 1 laxative
The total number of ways of drawing 2 tablets from the bottle (which contains 9 tablets) is
$$ \binom{9}{2}=36 $$
and since our selection is random, they are all equally likely.
Thus by Theorem $2.2\left(\frac{\text { # favourable }}{\text { # possible }}\right)$, the probability of drawing 1 aspirin and 1 laxative is
$$ \frac{\binom{3}{7}\binom{2}{0}\binom{4}{1}}{\binom{9}{2}}=\frac{12}{36}=\frac{1}{3} . $$
Continuing in this way, we get the following table
$x$
0 1 2
0 $\frac{1}{6}$ $\frac{1}{3}$ $\frac{1}{12}$
1 $\frac{2}{9}$ $\frac{1}{6}$
2 $\frac{1}{36}$
In fact, it is not too hard to see that we have the following formula
$$ P(X=x, Y=y)=\frac{\binom{3}{x}\binom{2}{y}\binom{4}{2-x-y}}{\binom{9}{2}}, \begin{aligned} & 0 \leq x, y \leq 2 \\ & 0 \leq x+y \leq 2 . \end{aligned} $$


Definition).

If $x, y$ are discrete random variables, the function $f(x, y)$ given by
$$ f(x, y)=P(X=x, Y=y) $$
for each pair of values $(x, y)$ in the range of $X, Y$ is called the joint probability distribution of $X$ and $Y$.
Like in Theorem 3.1 (for one random variable), it follows from the postulates of probability that:


Theorem 3.7

A bivariate function can serve as the joint PDF of a pair of discrete random variables $x, y$ if and only if its values $f(x, y)$ satisfy.
  1. $f(x, y) \geq 0$ for each pair of values $(x, y)$ in its domain;
  2. $\sum_{x} \sum_{y} f(x, y)=1$, the double sum being over all the values in the domain of $f$.


Example).

Find the value of $k$ for which
$$ f(x, y)=k x y, \quad x, y=1,2,3 . $$
can serve as a joint pdf.

Solution).

$f(x, y) \geq 0$ for each $x, y$ with $1 \leq x, y \leq 3$, so condition 1 is satisfied automatically.
To check condition 2, we want:
$$ \begin{aligned} & f(1,1)+f(1,2)+f(1,3)+f(2,1)+f(2,2)+f(2,1) \\ & +f(3,1)+f(3,2)+f(3,3)=1 \\ \\ \text{i.e. }& \\ \\ & k+2 k+3 k+2 k+4 k+6 k \\ & +3 k+6 k+9 k=1 \\ \\ & \text { i.e.} \quad 36 k=1, \\ & \text { so } \quad k=\frac{1}{36} . \end{aligned} $$


Definition).

If $x, y$ are discrete random variable, the function $F$ given by
$$ \begin{aligned} F(x, y)=P(X \leq x, Y \leq y)=\sum_{s \leq x} \sum_{t \leq y} f(s, t)\\ \text{for} \quad -\infty < x,y <\infty \end{aligned} $$
Where $f(s, t)$ is the Joint PDF of $X$ and $Y$ at $(3, t)$ is called the joint cumulative distribution function of $X$ and $Y$.


Theorem). 3.5.1

If $F(x, y)$ is the value of the point CDF of two discrete random variables $X \& Y$ at $(x, y)$, then

$$ a) \lim_{\substack{x\to -\infty\\ y\to -\infty}} F(x, y)=0 \quad \quad b) \lim_{\substack{x\to \infty\\ y\to \infty}} F(x, y)=1$$
$$c). \text{ if } a<b \text{ and } c<d \text{ then } F(a, c) \leq F(b, d)$$


Example).

For the tablets example, find $F(1,1),F(-2,1),F(3.7,4.5).$,

Solution)

$$ \begin{aligned} & F(1,1)=P(x \leq 1, y \leq 1) \\ &=f(0,0)+f(0,1)+f(1,0)+f(1,1) \\ &=\frac{1}{6}+\frac{2}{9}+\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{6} \\ &=\frac{8}{9} \\ \\ & F(-2,1)=P(x \leq-2, y \leq 1)=0 \\ & F(3.7,4.5)=P(x \leq 3.7, y \leq 4.5)=0 \end{aligned} $$

Note that as in the univeriate case, the CDF is defined for all real numbers not just the values assumed by $X$ & $Y$.
