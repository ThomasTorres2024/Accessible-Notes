In the last section we saw that if we could find all the derivatives $f^{(n)}(a)$ of $a g^{\circ} f(x)$ at $x=a$, then we can write down the Taylor series
$$ f(a)+f^{\prime}(a)(x-a)+\frac{f^{\prime \prime}(a)}{2!}(x-a)^{2}+\frac{f^{\prime \prime \prime}(a)}{3!}(x-a)^{3}+\cdots \cdot $$
for $f$ about $x=a$.
In practice, even when we know they exist, finding all the denvatives $f^{(n)}(a)$ can be very tedious. Howerer, there are some shortunts which work in certain special cases.

An Example from Physics.
An electric dipole consists of two charges $Q$ and $Q$ which are a distance $r$ apart.
The electric field at a point $P$ which is at distance $R$ frem the charge $Q$ as sham is given by
$$ E=\frac{Q}{R^{2}}-\frac{Q}{(R+r)^{2}} $$
Use series to investigate the behaviour of the clectric field far away from the dipule. Show that when $R \gg r$, the electric field is approximately proportional to $\frac{1}{R^{3}}$.
In order to use a series approximation, we need a variable whose value is small.
We know that $R$ is large while $r$ is fixed but arbitrary.
Harever, we also lenar that $R \gg \Gamma$, so $\frac{r}{R}$ is small. This suggests we use $r / R$ as our variable and we carry out some algebraic manipulation to make this possible.
$$ \frac{1}{(R+r)^{2}}=\frac{1}{R^{2}(1+r / R)^{2}}=\frac{1}{R^{2}}(1+r / R)^{-2} . $$
We now use the binmial series for $(1+x)^{2}$ with $\rho=-2$ and $x=r / R$ :
$$ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{R^{2}}(1+r / R)^{2}= & \frac{1}{R^{2}}\left(1-2\left(\frac{r}{R}\right)+\frac{(-2)(-3)}{2!}\left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^{2}\right. \\ & \left.+\frac{(-2)(-3)(-4)}{3!}\left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^{3}+\cdots\right) \end{aligned} $$
$$ =\frac{1}{R^{2}}\left(1-\frac{2 r}{R}+3 \frac{r^{2}}{R^{2}}-4 \frac{r^{3}}{R^{3}}+\cdots\right) $$
If we now substitute this into the expression fer the electric field, $E$, we qod.
$$ \begin{aligned} E & =\frac{Q}{R^{2}}-\frac{Q}{(R+r)^{2}} \\ & =Q\left[\frac{1}{R^{2}}-\frac{1}{R^{2}}\left(1-\frac{2 r}{R}+\frac{3 r^{2}}{R^{2}}-\frac{4 r^{3}}{R^{3}}+\cdots\right)\right] \\ & =\frac{Q}{R^{2}}\left(\frac{2 r}{R}-\frac{3 r^{2}}{R^{2}}+\frac{4 r^{3}}{R^{3}}-.\right) \end{aligned} $$
Note that this binomial expanson will converge since $\left|\frac{C}{R}\right| \ll 1$ (remember that the series expansion for $(1+x)^{\beta}$ is ralid for $-1<x<1$ ).
For $R$ very large, $\frac{F}{R}$ is very small and the leading term in the series will be large compared to the other terms (and also to their sum) and so
$$ E \approx \frac{Q}{R^{2}} \cdot \frac{2 r}{R}=\frac{2 Q_{r}}{R^{3}} $$
Thus for $R$ large, $E$ is approx. proportional to $\frac{1}{R^{3}}$ as required.
