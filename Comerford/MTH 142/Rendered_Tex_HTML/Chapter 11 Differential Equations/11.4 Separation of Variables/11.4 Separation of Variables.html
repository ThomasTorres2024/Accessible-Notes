$\S 11.4$ Separation of Variables

Recall that in $\S 11.2$ we looked the Differntial Equation:
$$ \frac{d y}{d x}=-\frac{x}{y} $$
and guessed that the solutions were circles of the form
$$ x^{2}+y^{2}=C $$
One can check that these are solutions by differentration:
$$ \begin{gathered} \frac{d}{d x}\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)=\frac{d}{d x}(C) \\ 2 x+2 y \frac{d y}{d x}=0 \\ 2 y \frac{d y}{d x}=-2 x \\ \frac{d y}{d x}=-\frac{x}{y} \end{gathered} $$
However, there is also a way to get the solution from the original equation.
$$ \frac{d y}{d x}=-\frac{x}{y} $$
via an algebraic trick.


The Algebraic Trick

$\frac{d y}{d x}$ like a fraction and rearrange the equation so that everything involving $y$ is on the left and everything involving $x$ is on the right
$$ \begin{aligned} & d y=-\frac{x}{y} d x \\ & y d y=-x d x \end{aligned} $$
This trick is known as the separation of variables (for obvious reasons!).
We now integrate both sides of the rearranged equation:
$$ \begin{aligned} & \int y d y=\int-x d x \\ & \frac{y^{2}}{2}=-\frac{x^{2}}{2}+k, \quad k \text { constant } \\ & y^{2}=-x^{2}+C \quad(C=2 k) \\ & x^{2}+y^{2}=C \end{aligned} $$
N.b. this trick doesn't always work one can't always separate $x$ and $y$. Neverthess, it is a very useful short cut when it does work.


Exponential Growth and Decay

Consider the DE
$$ \frac{d y}{d x}=k y $$
Separating the variables gives
$$ \frac{d y}{y}=k d x $$
We then integrate both sides
$$ \begin{aligned} & \int \frac{d y}{y}=\int k d x \\ & \ln |y|=k x+c, \quad c \text { constant. } \end{aligned} $$

Taking the exponential of both sides
$$ e^{\ln |y|}=e^{k x+c} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} & |y|=e^{k x+c} \\ & |y|=e^{k x} \cdot e^{c} \end{aligned} $$
So
$$ y= \pm e^{c} e^{k x} $$
and if we let $A$ be the constant $\pm e^{c}$, we get:


$$ y=A e^{k x} $$


If in addition, we want our solution to have a particular value $y_{0}$ at $x=0$, we have, on setting $x=0$,
<
$$ \begin{aligned} & y_{0}=A e^{k 00} \\ & y_{0}=A \cdot 1 \end{aligned} $$


Thus $A=y_{0}$ and the solution of the IVP is:

$$ y=y_{0} e^{k x} $$

If $k>0$, the solution grows without bound as $x \rightarrow \infty$ and we have exponential growth
If $k<0$, the solution tends to 0 as $x \rightarrow \infty$ and we have exponential decay


Finally, if $k=0$, then our solution is just the constant for

$$ y=y_{0} . $$


Example:

For $k>0$, find solutions of

$$ \frac{d H}{d t}=-k(H-20) $$

Separating variables gives

$$ \frac{d H}{H-20}=-k d t $$

and if we then integrate both sides, we get

$$ \begin{aligned} & \int \frac{d H}{H-20}=\int-k d t \\ & \ln |H-20|=-k t+C, \quad C \text { constant } \\ & e^{\ln |H-20|}=e^{-k t+C} \\ & |H-20|=e^{c} \cdot e^{-k t} \\ & H-20= \pm e^{c} \cdot e^{-k t} \end{aligned} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} & H-20=B e^{-k b} \quad \text { where } B= \pm e^{c} \\ & H=20+B e^{-k t} \end{aligned} $$

Solutions look like

An equation of this type could be used to model a situation where an object reaches thermal equilibrium (e.g. a beer left out in the garden when the temp. is $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ).


Example:

$\quad \frac{d P}{d t}=2 P-2 P t, \quad P(0)=5$
$$ =P(2-2 t) $$

Separating variables gives

$$ \frac{d p}{p}=(2-2 t) d t $$

Integrate:

$$ \begin{aligned} \int \frac{d p}{p} & =\int(2-2 t) d t \\ \ln |p| & =2 t-t^{2}+c, \quad c \text { constant. } \\ |P| & =e^{2 t-t^{2}+c} \\ P & = \pm e^{c} \cdot e^{2 t-t^{2}} \\ P & =A e^{2 t-t^{2}} \end{aligned} $$

Now use the $1 \mathrm{C} \quad P(0)=5$
$$ \begin{aligned} & 5=A e^{2(c)-0^{2}} \\ & 5=A \end{aligned} $$
So $p=5 e^{2 t-t^{2}}$
$$ =5 e^{1-(1-t)^{2}} $$
Solution is a bell-shaped curve (similar to the normal distribution in statistics) with the peak at $t=1$.



Example:

$$\large \frac{d y}{d t}=y^{2}(1+t), \quad y(1)=2$$.

Separate the variables
$$ \frac{d y}{y^{2}}=(1+t) d t $$
Integrate
$$ \begin{aligned} & \int \frac{d y}{y^{2}}=\int(1+t) d t \\ & -\frac{1}{y}=t+\frac{t^{2}}{2}+c \\ & y=\frac{-1}{t+t^{2} / 2+c} \end{aligned} $$
Apply the IC (initial condition) to find C
$$ y(1)=2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2=\frac{-1}{1+\frac{1}{2}+c} $$
$$ \begin{aligned} & -\frac{1}{2}=\frac{3}{2}+C \\ & C=-2 \end{aligned} $$
The solution to the IVP is then
$$ \begin{aligned} y & =\frac{1}{t+t^{2} / 2-2} \\ & =\frac{1}{2-t-t^{2} / 2} \\ & =\frac{2}{4-2 t-t^{2}} \end{aligned} $$


Justification for Separation of Variables

Suppose we can write our $D E$ in the form
$$ \frac{d y}{d x}=g(x) f(y) $$
Provided $f(y) \neq 0$, we can define $h(y)=\frac{1}{f(y)}$ and rewrite the equation as
$$ \frac{d y}{d x}=\frac{g(x)}{h(y)} $$
Now multiply both sides by $h(y)$
$$ h(y) \frac{d y}{d x}=g(x) $$
Next integrate both sides with respect to $x$.
$$ \int h(y) \text{ } \frac{dy}{dx} =\int g(x) d x $$
If we regard $y$ as a function of $x$, then for the left hand integral, we can treat $y$ as a substitution since
$$ d y=y^{\prime}(x) d x=\frac{d y}{d x} \cdot d x . $$
Using this we can then rewrite the equation as
$$ \int h(y) d y=\int g(x) d x $$
which is the same as we would have got (much more quickly) from separation of variables.
